In the recent years, the issue of CO2 emission has become a big topic in the world, presenting a significant challenge for politicians and environmental advocates. Like many regions worldwide, the European nations have worked hard to decrease CO2 emission to reduce the effects of climate change.

A Historical Analysis of CO2 Emissions in Europe

The journey into understanding CO2 emission begins with looking into historical data, shown through a line plot. The line plot showcases the CO2 emission from 1960 to 2022, revealing a remarkable increase in 1979 and a notable decrease afterwards with the lowest levels of CO2 emission in 2020

Europe's CO2 Emission Journey (1960-2022)

Taking a trip down memory lane

In 1979, a huge increase in CO2 emission occured. The second oil crisis happened in 1979, which was an aftermaath of the Iranian revolution. It was the disruption in oil production from Iran that caused the decline in global supply. Because of the shortage of oil and increase of energy prices, the world started to shift the energy production away from oil to coal. The transition meant that CO2 emission increased, because coal produces more CO2 emissions per unit of energy compared to oil. The second oil crisis was a global issue, where EU countries heavily relied on oil imports for energy, transportation, and industry. Now if we take a closer look into 1979 through a map plot, we see that countries such as Germany, Poland and France had high CO2 emission, where Germany had the highest CO2 emissions.

Breaking down 1979

In 1979, Germany faced some factors that contributed to its high CO2 emissions. At that time, Germany highly relied on coal for its energy production, which stemmed from a combination of historical factors and the countries industrial landscape. As Germany is one of Europe's leading industrial powers, with it's strong manufacturing sector, including steel production, chemical manufacturing, and cement production, then it also makes sense that they have a quite high level of CO2 emissions compared to other countries.

A Pandemic Pause

Notably in 2020, where the CO2 emissions hit the lowest level in EU. In 2020 the world was hit by a pandemic known as COVID 19, which slowed down the global economy, resulting in a decrease in CO2 emissions.

{% include CO2_Emission_EU_Map_Online_2020.html %} Europe's CO2 Emission in 2020

Due to the quarantines in the covid 19 pandemic, changes in consumer behavior and lifestyle choices occured, which influenced energy consumption patterns and emission levels. Many people shifted towards remote work, which meant a reduction in transportation resulting in reduction in CO2 emission levels. When we once again take a look into the CO2 emission in 2020, we see that Germany is once again the country with the highest CO2 emission, while Malta is once again the country with the lowest level of CO2 emissions. Even though the two countrie's spots remain the same, we still do see a decrease of CO2 emissions in both countries, which makes sense based on the circumstances around those events.

Steps Towards Reduction

Even though Germany still maintain its position as the EU country with the highest CO2 emission level, there has been a noticecable decrease over the years, which can be seen through the line plot. This decline reflects the efforts in adressing the issue of climate change.

Germany has implemented a series of initiatives aimed to reduce CO2 emissions to transition towards a more sustainable energy landscape. Germany has decide to increase the pricing for the transport sector and for heating buildings They are actively promoting the use of energy-saving buildings, renewables and alternative technology in the transportation sector, such as electric mobility. In 2021, they changed the pricing of fossil fuels such as oil, gas, petrol and diesel, so companies were required to pay a CO2 price. As for 2019 about 43 percent of electric power was generated from renewable soruces, such as wind and solar power. Since 2020, home owners have also had the opportunity to receive a payment for replacing old oil central heating, further reducing the CO2 emissions.

Moreover, Germany has been exploring and implementing a series of initiatives, showing the strong commitment to reduce CO2 emissions. Currently, their plan is to reduce greenhouse gas emission by 55 percent by 2030, by stop using coal to generate electic power and restructure their mobility.

Shaping a future

As we navigate the complexities of the 21st century, it's important to recognize the historical events and the CO2 emissions. By learning from the past and getting insights from the historical analysis, we can get a deeper understanding of how future events might impact CO2 emissions. With this knowledge we are better equopped to anticipate the challenges and also opportunites that lie ahead of us. Trough strategic policy interventions, investments in renewable energy and adaption measures, we can move towards a more sustainable future. By investing in renewable energy infrastructure, such as solar and wind power or prioritizing adaption measures such as transitioning to low-carbon transportation option, can help lower the CO2 emission levels.

Ultimately, the lessions drawn from Europ's CO2 emission history serve as a reminder to take action.
