This plot illustrates that storms, extreme temperatures, and floods are the most frequent natural disasters. However, there isn't a clear trend of decreasing occurrences across different types of disasters; instead, we observe fluctuations with peaks and declines.

Floods reached their peak in 2005 with 39 occurrences, but this peak hasn't been surpassed since. However, it's challenging to determine whether floods are decreasing or increasing, as seen in years like 2021, where there were 28 occurrences, compared to 15 the year before and 4 the year after.

Extreme temperatures peaked in 2023 and haven't reached that level since. Since then, extreme temperatures have shown fewer occurrences overall, but the significant increase in 2022 suggests a fluctuating pattern rather than a consistent decrease over time.

Storms peaked in 2023 with 31 occurrences, with most years not having fewer than 5 occurrences. Exceptions include 2012, with only one storm globally in the EU, and 2016, with two storms globally in the EU.

When considering this information alongside the EU emission data, which shows a general decrease but fluctuates between being higher or lower each year, we observe an overall decrease when comparing the initial and final years. However, there isn't a consistent trend throughout the intervening years. Overall, the occurrences of natural disasters also follow a decreasing trend similar to EU emissions, though not as significantly.

Exploring Europe's Top Emitters

According to Figure Three in the first blog post, Germany is identified as the country with the highest CO2 emissions in the EU. Click on each disaster type to remove them from the plot and investigate if there are any instances of overlapping disaster types.
{% include Germany_EU.html %}
Germany encounters a variety of 5 natural disasters, with only one instance of an earthquake and two of an epidemic occurring between 2002 and 2004. Storms are the most frequent natural disaster in Germany, peaking multiple times at three occurrences throughout the years. Extreme temperatures are also observed multiple times, reaching a peak of two occurrences. Flooding is present across the years, peaking in 2005 with two instances.
Interestingly, the plot from the first blog post reveals that in 2020, Germany experienced its lowest CO2 emissions, while globally, it represented the highest emissions.
Despite this, according to our data, Germany only encountered one natural disaster in the form of a storm in 2020. Comparatively, in 2015, when no disasters were detected, Germany's CO2 emissions were higher than in 2020. Although a direct link to each year may not be evident, it is noteworthy that Germany experienced at least one natural disaster annually, while being the largest CO2 emitter in the EU. {% include Italy_EU.html%} Italy experiences a significantly higher number of natural disasters compared to Germany, both in terms of quantity and variety. There is a greater diversity of natural disasters occurring in Italy. Figure 3 illustrates an increasing trend in Italy's natural disasters leading up to the 2000s, peaking in 2005. However, the occurrence of natural disasters in 2005 does not align with this trend.
Interestingly, while Italy's CO2 emissions have been steadily decreasing since their peak in 2005, there is a subsequent increase in the number of natural disasters observed from around year 2014. Storms, in particular, have become more prevalent since 2014, a trend not previously observed. Conversely, flooding occurrences have decreased since 2014 compared to earlier years, when Italy predominantly experienced flood disasters.

In summary, we observe that while Italy's emissions were at their peak in 2005, there wasn't a corresponding increase in natural disasters. However, as emissions began to decrease, we noticed an uptick in natural disasters. This suggests that the effects and consequences of CO2 emissions may be felt and manifested years after the emissions occur.
